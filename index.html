Retinol 101 – What It Is and How It Works

Retinol is a form of vitamin A. Once applied to the skin, enzymes slowly convert it into retinoic acid — the active molecule that changes gene expression in skin cells. This influences cell turnover, collagen support and pigment distribution over time.

Because this conversion takes time, cosmetic retinol is usually less irritating than prescription tretinoin. But it can still cause dryness, peeling and burning when introduced too aggressively.

What retinol can realistically do

  • Smooth mild uneven texture and fine lines over months.
  • Help fade post-acne marks and some hyperpigmentation.
  • Support collagen with consistent long-term use.
  • Reduce clogged pores and comedonal acne in some people.

What retinol cannot do

  • Replace sunscreen — UV protection remains essential.
  • Erase deep folds, sagging or severe sun damage.
  • Work overnight — meaningful changes take months.
  • Guarantee identical results for every skin type.

Retinol vs Retinal vs Tretinoin

All three are vitamin A derivatives, but they sit at different points in the conversion chain to retinoic acid. The closer you are to retinoic acid, the faster — and often harsher — the effect.

Cosmetic retinol

Needs two conversion steps. Slower and generally gentler. Ideal for beginners and long-term maintenance when paired with daily SPF.

Retinal (retinaldehyde)

Needs one conversion step. Often faster than retinol at similar percentages. A strong “next step” once retinol tolerance is stable.

Tretinoin

Prescription retinoic acid. Works directly and can deliver faster change, but with a higher irritation risk. Best used under medical supervision.

How to Build a Beginner-Friendly Retinol Routine

Most barrier problems come from doing too much, too soon. A conservative approach usually beats “max strength from day one”.

  1. Prepare: cleanse with a mild, non-stripping cleanser and gently pat dry.
  2. Wait: allow skin to dry completely (10–15 minutes) to reduce stinging.
  3. Apply: use a pea-sized amount for the whole face, avoiding eye area and corners of nose and mouth.
  4. Seal: follow with a simple, fragrance-free moisturiser.
  5. Frequency: start with 2 nights per week for at least 2–3 weeks before increasing.

Very reactive or nervous? Use the “sandwich method” — moisturiser → small amount of retinol → another layer of moisturiser — or start with once weekly.

Side Effects and When to Stop

Mild dryness, tightness and light flaking are common when you first introduce retinol. These can usually be managed by reducing frequency and moisturising more.

Often manageable

  • Slight dryness or tight feeling.
  • Light flaking around nose and mouth.
  • Small breakouts (“purging”) in congested areas.

Warning signs – pause use

  • Burning or stinging that persists more than a few minutes.
  • Red, shiny “plastic” looking skin.
  • Cracks and soreness around eyes, nose or mouth.

If you see warning signs, stop all retinoids until your skin feels normal again. Simplify your routine to a gentle cleanser, bland moisturiser and daily sunscreen.

Who Should Be Extra Careful or Avoid Retinol?

  • Pregnant or breastfeeding people — discuss vitamin A products with your doctor.
  • Those with uncontrolled eczema, rosacea or highly reactive skin.
  • Anyone using strong prescription treatments like oral isotretinoin.

In these situations, self-experimenting based on general internet advice is risky. A dermatologist who knows your medical history is in a better position to guide you.

Retinol FAQ

Can I use retinol around my eyes?

Some people tolerate retinol around the eyes, others do not. If you try it, start by applying a tiny amount over a layer of eye cream, once per week. Dedicated eye formulas with milder retinoids are often safer.

Can teenagers use retinol?

Teens with acne are more often prescribed adapalene or tretinoin under medical supervision. Cosmetic retinol is usually not necessary unless a professional recommends it.

Is “purging” normal?

Retinoids can speed up the life cycle of clogged pores, which may temporarily increase breakouts in the first weeks. This should gradually calm down. If breakouts are widespread, painful or continue for months, see a dermatologist.
